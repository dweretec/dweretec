<p>A <b>religion</b> is a system of human thought which usually includes a set of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myth" title="Myth" class="mw-redirect">narratives</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a>, beliefs and practices that give meaning to the practitioner's experiences of life through reference to a higher power, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> or deities, or ultimate <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Religion is commonly identified by the practitioner's <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayer</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, among other things, but more generally is interwoven with <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>. It may focus on specific <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a>, and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">moral</a> claims about <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a> (the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">cosmos</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">human nature</a>) which may yield a set of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_law" title="Religious law">religious laws</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, and a particular <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifestyle" title="Lifestyle">lifestyle</a>. Religion also encompasses ancestral or cultural <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">traditions</a>, writings, history, and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>, as well as personal <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith" title="Faith">faith</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_experience" title="Religious experience">religious experience</a>.</p>
<p>The term "religion" refers to both the personal practices related to communal faith and to group rituals and communication stemming from shared conviction. "Religion" is sometimes used interchangeably with "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith" title="Faith">faith</a>" or "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief_system" title="Belief system">belief system</a>,"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> but it is more socially defined than personal convictions, and it entails specific <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_behaviour" title="Religious behaviour">behaviors</a>, respectively.</p>

<p>The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Development_of_religion" title="Development of religion">development of religion</a> has taken many forms in various cultures. It considers <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology_of_religion" title="Psychology of religion">psychological</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_religion" title="Sociology of religion">social</a> roots, along with <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origins_of_religion" title="Origins of religion" class="mw-redirect">origins</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_religion" title="History of religion" class="mw-redirect">historical</a> development.</p>
<p>In the frame of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_religion" title="Western religion">western religious thought</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> religions present a common quality, the "hallmark of patriarchal religious thought": the division of the world in two comprehensive domains, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred-profane_dichotomy" title="Sacred-profane dichotomy">one sacred, the other profane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Religion is often described as a communal system for the coherence of belief focusing on a system of thought, unseen being, person, or object, that is considered to be <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>, sacred, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divinity" title="Divinity">divine</a>, or of the highest truth. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_code" title="Moral code" class="mw-redirect">Moral codes</a>, practices, values, institutions, tradition, rituals, and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scripture" title="Scripture">scriptures</a> are often traditionally associated with the core belief, and these may have some overlap with concepts in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularity" title="Secularity">secular</a> <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. Religion is also often described as a "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifestyle" title="Lifestyle">way of life</a>" or a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_stance" title="Life stance">life stance</a>.</p>
