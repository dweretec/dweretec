<p><b>Economic growth</b> is the increase of per capita <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) or other measure of aggregate income. It is often measured as the rate of change in real GDP. Economic growth refers only to the quantity of goods and services produced.</p>
<p>Economic growth can be either positive or negative. Negative growth can be referred to by saying that the economy is <i>shrinking</i>. Negative growth is associated with economic <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> and economic depression.</p>
<p>In order to compare per capita income among countries, the statistics may be quoted in a single currency, based on either prevailing <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange_rates" title="Exchange rates" class="mw-redirect">exchange rates</a> or <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a>. To compensate for changes in the value of money (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> or deflation) the GDP or GNP is usually given in "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_vs._nominal_in_economics" title="Real vs. nominal in economics" class="mw-redirect">real</a>" or inflation adjusted, terms rather than the actual money figure compiled in a given year, which is called the nominal or current figure.</p>
<p>Economists draw a distinction between short-term economic stabilization and long-term economic growth. The topic of economic growth is primarily concerned with the long run. The short-run variation of economic growth is termed the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle</a>.</p>
<p>The long-run path of economic growth is one of the central questions of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>; despite some problems of measurement, an increase in GDP of a country is generally taken as an increase in the standard of living of its inhabitants. Over long periods of time, even small rates of annual growth can have large effects through compounding (see <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a>). A growth rate of 2.5% per annum will lead to a doubling of GDP within 29 years, whilst a growth rate of 8% per annum (experienced by some <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Asian_Tigers" title="Four Asian Tigers">Four Asian Tigers</a>) will lead to a doubling of GDP within 10 years. This exponential characteristic can exacerbate differences across nations.</p>
