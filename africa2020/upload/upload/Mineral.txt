<p>A <b>mineral</b> is a naturally occurring <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> that is formed through <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> processes and that has a characteristic <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition" class="mw-redirect">chemical composition</a>, a highly ordered <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_structure" title="Atomic structure" class="mw-redirect">atomic structure</a>, and specific <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical properties</a>. By comparison, a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> is an <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aggregate" title="Aggregate">aggregate</a> of minerals and/or <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineraloid" title="Mineraloid">mineraloids</a> and does not have a specific chemical composition. Minerals range in composition from pure <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> and simple <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> to very complex <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a> with thousands of known forms.<sup id="cite_ref-Klein_0-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral#cite_note-Klein-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The study of minerals is called <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a>.</p>
