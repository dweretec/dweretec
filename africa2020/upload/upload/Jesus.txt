<p><b>Jesus of Nazareth</b> (<i>c</i> 4 <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ" class="mw-redirect">BC</a>/<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Before_Common_Era" title="Before Common Era" class="mw-redirect">BCE</a> – <i>c</i> 30 <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a>/<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-Sanders_0-2" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-Sanders-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>—also known as <b>Jesus <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a></b> or occasionally <b>Jesus the Christ</b>—is the central figure of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. Within most <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">Christian denominations</a> Jesus is venerated as the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a> and as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">God incarnate</a>. Christians also view him as the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> foretold in the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>; however, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_views_of_Jesus" title="Jewish views of Jesus" class="mw-redirect">rejects</a> these claims. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> considers <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">Jesus</a> a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prophets_in_Islam" title="Prophets in Islam" class="mw-redirect">prophet</a> and also the Messiah<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> while several other religions <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverence" title="Reverence">revere</a> him in some way.</p>
<p>The principal sources of information regarding Jesus' life and teachings are the four <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_gospels" title="Canonical gospels" class="mw-redirect">canonical gospels</a>, especially the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synoptic_Gospels" title="Synoptic Gospels">Synoptic Gospels</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ActJIntro_5-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-ActJIntro-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> though some scholars argue that other texts (such as the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gospel_of_Thomas" title="Gospel of Thomas">Gospel of Thomas</a>) are as relevant as the canonical gospels to the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_Jesus" title="Historical Jesus">historical Jesus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-levine_6-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-levine-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Most critical scholars in the fields of history and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_studies" title="Biblical studies">biblical studies</a> believe that some parts of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> are useful for reconstructing Jesus' life,<sup id="cite_ref-5GIntro_7-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-5GIntro-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harris_HJ_8-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-Harris_HJ-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EJ_9-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-EJ-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> agreeing that he was a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jew</a> who was regarded as a teacher and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">healer</a>. They also generally accept that he was <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> by <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, and was <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">crucified</a> in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> on the orders of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">Roman Prefect</a> of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iudaea_Province" title="Iudaea Province">Judaea</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontius_Pilate" title="Pontius Pilate">Pontius Pilate</a>, on the charge of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedition" title="Sedition">sedition</a> against the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Aside from these few conclusions, academic studies remain inconclusive about the chronology, the central message of Jesus' preaching, his social class, cultural environment, and religious orientation.<sup id="cite_ref-levine_6-1" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-levine-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Scholars offer competing descriptions of Jesus as the awaited Messiah,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> as a self-described Messiah, as the leader of an apocalyptic movement, as an itinerant sage, as a charismatic healer, and as the founder of an independent religious movement.</p>
<p><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_views_of_Jesus" title="Christian views of Jesus">Christian views of Jesus</a> (see also <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a>) center on the belief that Jesus is <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">divine</a>, is the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Messiah</a> whose coming was <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claimed_Messianic_prophecies_of_Jesus" title="Claimed Messianic prophecies of Jesus" class="mw-redirect">prophesied in the Old Testament</a>, and that he was <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">resurrected</a> after his crucifixion. Theologian and bishop <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesslie_Newbigin" title="Lesslie Newbigin">Lesslie Newbigin</a> says "the whole of Christian teaching would fall to the ground if it were the case that the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus were not events in real history but stories told to illustrate truths which are valid apart from these happenings."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Christians do predominantly believe that Jesus is the "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a>" (generally meaning that he is <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">God the Son</a>, the second person in the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>) who came to provide <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvation#Christianity" title="Salvation">salvation</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confession" title="Confession">reconciliation</a> with <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> by his death for their <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sin#Christian_views_of_sin" title="Sin">sins</a>. Other Christian beliefs include Jesus' <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgin_birth_of_Jesus" title="Virgin birth of Jesus">virgin birth</a>, performance of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miracles_attributed_to_Jesus" title="Miracles attributed to Jesus">miracles</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">ascension</a> into <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a>, and a future <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a>. While the doctrine of the Trinity is widely accepted by most Christians, a few groups <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">reject as non-scriptural</a>, wholly or partly, the doctrine of the Trinity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Islam, Jesus (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">عيسى</span>‎, commonly transliterated as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">Isa</a>) is considered one of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God's</a> important <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prophets_in_Islam" title="Prophets in Islam" class="mw-redirect">prophets</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> a bringer of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injil" title="Injil">scripture</a>, and a worker of miracles. Jesus is also called "Messiah", but Islam does not teach that he was divine. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_view_of_Jesus%27_death" title="Islamic view of Jesus' death">Islam teaches</a> that Jesus ascended bodily to heaven without experiencing the crucifixion and resurrection,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> rather than the traditional Christian belief of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_and_resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Death and resurrection of Jesus">death and resurrection of Jesus</a>.</p>
