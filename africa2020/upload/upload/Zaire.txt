<p>The <b>Republic of Zaire</b> (pronounced <span title="Pronunciation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:IPA_for_English" title="Wikipedia:IPA for English">/zɑːˈɪər/</a></span>; <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>République du Zaïre</i></span> <span title="Pronunciation in IPA" class="IPA"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:IPA_for_French" title="Wikipedia:IPA for French">[za.iʁ]</a></span>) was the name of the present <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> between 27 October 1971, and 17 May 1997. The name of Zaire derives from the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>Zaire</i></span>, itself an adaptation of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">Kongo</a> word <i>nzere</i> or <i>nzadi</i>, or "the river that swallows all rivers".<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaire#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Known as the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a> up until its independence in June 1960, unrest and rebellion plagued the new government until 1965, when Lieutenant General <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Joseph-Désiré Mobutu</a>, by then commander-in-chief of the national army, seized control of the country and declared himself president for five years during what is now called the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congo_Crisis" title="Congo Crisis">Congo Crisis</a>. Mobutu quickly consolidated his power and was elected unopposed as president in 1970.</p>
