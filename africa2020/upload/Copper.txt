<p><b>Copper</b> is a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Cu</b> (from <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language" class="mw-redirect">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>cuprum</i></span>) and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 29. It is a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a> metal with very high <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity" class="mw-redirect">electrical conductivity</a>. Pure copper is soft and malleable; a freshly exposed surface has a reddish-orange color. It is used as a conductor of heat and electricity, a building material, and a constituent of various metal <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>.</p>
<p>The metal and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_copper_alloys" title="List of copper alloys">its alloys</a> have been used for thousands of years. In the Roman era, copper was principally mined on <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, hence the origin of the name of the metal as <i>сyprium</i> (metal of Cyprus), later shortened to <i>сuprum</i>. Its compounds are commonly encountered as copper(II) salts, which often impart blue or green colors to minerals such as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azurite" title="Azurite">azurite</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a> and have been widely used historically as pigments. Architectural structures built with copper corrode to give green <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verdigris" title="Verdigris">verdigris</a> (or <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a>). <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decorative_art" title="Decorative art" class="mw-redirect">Decorative art</a> prominently features copper, both by itself and as part of pigments.</p>
<p>Copper is essential to all living organisms as a trace <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_mineral" title="Dietary mineral" class="mw-redirect">dietary mineral</a> because it is a key constituent of the respiratory enzyme complex <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_c_oxidase" title="Cytochrome c oxidase">cytochrome c oxidase</a>. In <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs" class="mw-redirect">molluscs</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacea" title="Crustacea" class="mw-redirect">crustacea</a> copper is a constituent of the blood pigment <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemocyanin" title="Hemocyanin">hemocyanin</a>, which is replaced by the iron-complexed <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> in fish and other <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>. The main areas where copper is found in humans are liver, muscle and bone.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Copper compounds are used as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriostatic_agent" title="Bacteriostatic agent">bacteriostatic substances</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicides</a>, and wood preservatives.</p>
