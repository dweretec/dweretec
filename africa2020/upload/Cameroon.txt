Cameroon</b> (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>République du Cameroun</i></span>), (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Republik Kamerun</i></span>), is a country in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>. It is bordered by <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> to the west; <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> to the northeast; the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> to the east; and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, and the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> to the south. Cameroon's coastline lies on the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bight_of_Bonny" title="Bight of Bonny">Bight of Bonny</a>, part of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a> and the Atlantic Ocean. The country is often referred to as "Africa in miniature" for its geological and cultural diversity. Natural features include beaches, deserts, mountains, rainforests, and savannas. The highest point is <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a> in the southwest, and the largest cities are <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douala" title="Douala">Douala</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaound%C3%A9" title="Yaoundé">Yaoundé</a>, and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garoua" title="Garoua">Garoua</a>. Cameroon is home to over 200 different linguistic groups. The country is well known for its native styles of music, particularly <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makossa" title="Makossa">makossa</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bikutsi" title="Bikutsi">bikutsi</a>, and for its successful <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon_national_football_team" title="Cameroon national football team">national football team</a>. French and English are the official languages. After independence, the newly united nation joined the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>, although the vast majority of its territories had previously been a German colony and, after <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, a French mandate.</p>
<p>Early inhabitants of the territory included the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sao_civilisation" title="Sao civilisation">Sao civilisation</a> around <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Chad" title="Lake Chad">Lake Chad</a> and the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baka_(Cameroon_and_Gabon)" title="Baka (Cameroon and Gabon)" class="mw-redirect">Baka</a> hunter-gatherers in the southeastern rainforest. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">Portuguese explorers</a> reached the coast in the 15th century and named the area <i>Rio dos Camarões</i> (<i>Shrimp River</i>), which became <i>Cameroon</i> in English. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fula_people" title="Fula people">Fulani</a> soldiers founded the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adamawa_Emirate" title="Adamawa Emirate">Adamawa Emirate</a> in the north in the 19th century, and various ethnic groups of the west and northwest established powerful chiefdoms and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fon_(Cameroon)" title="Fon (Cameroon)" class="mw-redirect">fondoms</a>. Cameroon became a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German colony</a> in 1884 known as Kamerun.</p>
<p>After World War I, the territory was divided between <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">Britain</a> as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">League of Nations mandates</a>. The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_of_the_Peoples_of_Cameroon" title="Union of the Peoples of Cameroon">Union des Populations du Cameroun</a> (UPC) political party advocated independence, but was outlawed by France in the 1950s. It waged war on French and UPC militant forces until 1971. In 1960, the French-administered part of Cameroon became independent as the Republic of Cameroun under President <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmadou_Ahidjo" title="Ahmadou Ahidjo">Ahmadou Ahidjo</a>. The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cameroons" title="Southern Cameroons">southern part</a> of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Cameroons" title="British Cameroons">British Cameroons</a> merged with it in 1961 to form the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_republic" title="Federal republic">Federal Republic</a> of Cameroon. The country was renamed the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">United Republic</a> of Cameroon in 1972 and the Republic of Cameroon in 1984.</p>
<p>Cameroon enjoys relatively high political and social stability. This has permitted the development of agriculture, roads, railways, and large petroleum and timber industries. Nevertheless, large numbers of Cameroonians live in poverty as subsistence farmers. Power lies firmly in the hands of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> president since 1982, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Biya" title="Paul Biya">Paul Biya</a>, and his <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameroon_People%27s_Democratic_Movement" title="Cameroon People's Democratic Movement">Cameroon People's Democratic Movement</a> party. The English-speaking territories of Cameroon have grown increasingly alienated from the government, and politicians from those regions have called for greater decentralization and even secession (for example: the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cameroons_National_Council" title="Southern Cameroons National Council">Southern Cameroons National Council</a>) of the former British-governed territories.</p>
