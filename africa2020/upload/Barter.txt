<p><b>Barter</b> is a system of exchange by which <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> or <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> are directly exchanged for other goods or services without using a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_of_exchange" title="Medium of exchange">medium of exchange</a>, such as <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barter#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is distinguishable from gift economies in many ways; one of them is that the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocity_(cultural_anthropology)" title="Reciprocity (cultural anthropology)">reciprocal exchange</a> is immediate and not delayed in time. It is usually <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bilateral" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:bilateral">bilateral</a>, but may be <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilateralism" title="Multilateralism">multilateral</a> (i.e., mediated through barter organizations) and usually exists parallel to monetary systems in most developed countries, though to a very limited extent. Barter usually replaces money as the method of exchange in times of monetary crisis, such as when the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> may be either unstable (e.g., <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a> or <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deflation#Deflationary_spiral" title="Deflation">deflationary spiral</a>) or simply unavailable for conducting commerce.</p>
<p>The inefficiency of barter in archaic society has been used by economists since <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> to explain the emergence of money, the economy, and hence the discipline of economics itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Graeber-p21-41_2-0" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barter#cite_note-Graeber-p21-41-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> However, no present or past society has ever been seen through <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnographic" title="Ethnographic" class="mw-redirect">ethnographic</a> studies to use pure barter without any medium of exchange, nor the emergence of money from barter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barter#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1830s, direct barter in western market economies has been aided by exchanges which frequently utilize alternative currencies based on the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_theory_of_value" title="Labour theory of value" class="mw-redirect">labour theory of value</a>, and designed to prevent profit taking by intermediators. Examples include the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owenite" title="Owenite" class="mw-redirect">Owenite</a> socialists, the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cincinnati_Time_Store" title="Cincinnati Time Store">Cincinnati Time store</a>, and more recently <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ithaca_Hours" title="Ithaca Hours">Ithaca HOURS</a> (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_banking" title="Time banking" class="mw-redirect">Time banking</a>) and the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LETS_system" title="LETS system" class="mw-redirect">LETS system</a>.</p>
